trail level approximately **$144.26** (peak $159.51 minus locked trail
$15.25), locking in **≥+4.8% minimum exit**.

The contrast with AXTI is the entire point: AXTI's bracket TP fired at $101.19
in the first 10 minutes of the gap-up bar, *before* the trailing manager's
next evaluation; SNOW's gap landed the morning price between activation and
TP, so the trail won the race. **Same system, same parameters, different
arrival sequence at one specific time-of-day.** That conditional is what the
new "trailing crowded out by TP" enhancement entry in CLAUDE.md needs to
reflect — not "the trail never works," but "the trail's success depends on
the price being between activation and TP at the moment the once-per-day
manager runs."

## 6. What this means for the "trailing crowded out by TP" CLAUDE.md entry

The CLAUDE.md enhancement entry added 2026-05-16 (from AXTI) is correct but
under-specified. SNOW refines it:

**Original framing**: "On fast moves the bracket TP fires before the trail
can engage."

**Refined framing**: "On **overnight gap moves** that take the price from
below activation to above TP, the bracket TP fires first. On **intraday
moves** that take the price from below activation to between activation and
TP — and the manager runs while it's there — the trail converts. The
once-per-day evaluation cadence + the narrow activation→TP gap make trail
conversion conditional on a specific price-time alignment that AXTI missed
and SNOW caught."

This sharpens the proposed interventions (ranked):

1. **Intraday evaluation** — runs at e.g. 09:35 ET *and* 15:30 ET. Catches
   slow morning rallies that have already passed activation by close. Would
   have caught AXTI (post-9:30 ET fill at TP could have been pre-empted by a
   ~09:35 ET trail submission before the LMT triggered, *if* the manager ran
   before the TP). This is the most leverage per unit of code change.
2. **Widen the activation→TP gap** (raise `atr_take_profit_multiplier` from 3.0 to e.g. 4.0–5.0) — gives the manager more "room" to catch the price before the TP fires. AXTI's gap would have widened from 1.0×ATR ($9.83) to 2.0×ATR ($19.66), making TP roughly $111. Probably would not have helped on the specific 2026-05-04 sequence (open $97.44 already above activation, then rally to $102 — would still be in the same window) but would help in cases where the overnight gap doesn't punch through both levels.
3. **Lower `trailing_stop_activation_atr`** (2.0 → 1.0) — same suggestion as the AXTI entry. SNOW would have converted on 2026-05-01 close $143.01 instead of waiting for the 2026-05-07 explosion (activation would have been $137.65 + 1.0 × ATR ≈ $144 — close call but possibly converted that week). Locks in less of the eventual peak gain, but reduces dependence on lucky timing.

The intervention **ranking should be (1) intraday evaluation > (2) widen
gap > (3) lower activation**. The AXTI write-up had these in different order
because AXTI alone couldn't distinguish them.

## 7. What SNOW does NOT prove

- **Trail is not "broken"**: SNOW shows the trail can fire when timing
  aligns. n=1 success in the entire database is small but not zero.
- **Trail distance is fixed at conversion time**: $15.25 here. Today's ATR
  is $4.45, so the trail is effectively 3.4×ATR-today vs the design intent
  of 2.0×ATR. This is mechanically how IBKR TRAIL orders work (auxPrice is
  a fixed dollar amount), not a bug — but worth being explicit about: the
  trail does NOT tighten as volatility falls. SNOW is benefiting from this
  (volatility has fallen, the trail is wider in current-ATR terms, the
  position has more room).
- **The position is still open**: any future drawdown to ~$144 would close
  the trade at +4.8%. Any further rally extends the locked-in floor. The
  case study should be revisited when the trail eventually fires.

## 12. Skill vs luck retrospective

### 12.1 Did the system's risk machinery behave as designed?

**Yes, end-to-end.** The bracket entered with stop $123.13 and TP $159.43
on 2026-04-30. The trailing manager evaluated every trading day and
correctly held off conversion until 2026-05-07 (the first day price entered
the activation→TP window). The conversion correctly cancelled the bracket
legs and submitted a TRAIL order with the design-intended trail amount
(2.0×ATR at conversion time = $15.25). IBKR's internal trailing logic
ratcheted the trigger up as the price rose to $176.98 on 5/19. The
gap-down on 5/21 triggered the stop and the resulting market sell fired
within seconds — no orphan legs, no double exit, no manual cleanup needed.

The one observable behaviour worth flagging: **after the conversion,
neither the daily-runner trail manager nor the intraday runner re-evaluates
trail distance.** The trail distance is fixed at $15.25 (2.0×ATR-as-of-
conversion-day). ATR at exit was lower (under $5 in recent bars). This
is mechanically correct — IBKR TRAIL orders take a fixed `auxPrice` and
that's how they work — but the design implication is that a position
where volatility *falls* substantially after conversion ends up with a
trail that is **wider** than 2.0×ATR-today. In SNOW's case that wide trail
**helped** (gave more room to ride the move). In a future case where
volatility *rose* after conversion, the trail would be tighter than design.
The system has no mechanism to re-tighten or re-widen.

### 12.2 Was the exit reasonable vs the post-trail-conversion peak?

Conversion price 5/7: $154.81. Peak 5/19: $176.98. Exit (est.): $161.73.

| | Price | Gain from entry | Captured fraction |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conversion floor (5/7) | $139.56 (initial trail level) | +1.4% | Trail-floor at conversion was barely above entry — the floor became the realised floor only because the position never gave back to it |
| Peak (5/19 intraday) | $176.98 | +28.6% | "If we could have sold at the top" — counterfactual ceiling |
| Inferred exit (5/21 trail) | $161.73 | +17.5% | **61% of peak-move captured** |
| Bracket TP (5/7 alt-history) | $159.43 (would have fired had move been overnight) | +15.8% | If 5/7 had been a gap-up instead of intraday rally |

**The trail captured ~61% of the entry-to-peak move and locked in a
+15.7-point improvement over the alt-history bracket-TP exit at $159.43.**
That is the design intent of trailing stops working correctly.

The remaining 39% (peak $176.98 → exit $161.73) is the fixed $15.25 trail
distance — a known design tradeoff. A 1.0×ATR trail at conversion would
have been $7.62 and exited at ~$169.36 (+23.0% / +$9,196); a 3.0×ATR
trail would have been $22.87 and exited at ~$154.11 (+12.0% / +$4,773).
SNOW worked out well at 2.0×ATR but the choice is regime-dependent.

### 12.3 Counterfactuals

1. **`trailing_stop_trail_atr` 2.0 → 1.0** (tighter trail). Trail distance
   would have been $7.62. Exit on the first daily pullback that crossed
   peak − $7.62 — would have triggered on **5/18** (5/18 high $165.28
   minus trail = $157.66; 5/18 low $155.19 traversed it). Exit ~+14.2%
   instead of +17.5%. **Worse outcome** here, but better in a case where
   the peak is followed by a slower decline.
2. **`trailing_stop_trail_atr` 2.0 → 3.0** (looser trail). Trail distance
   $22.87. Trigger after 5/19 peak ratchets to $154.11. Exit price would
   depend on if/when the price hit $154.11 — in the next 1–2 weeks

4. **Intraday evaluation (12:00 ET / 15:30 ET in addition to 09:35 ET)**.
   The intraday runner shipped 2026-05-20 with conversions *disabled by

   IBKR before either runner's check. **Intraday evaluation does not
   help once the trail is established** — its value is for *converting*
   borderline cases (AXTI), not for harvesting better exits on converted
   positions. The 5/21 SNOW exit was harvested by IBKR's continuous
   monitoring of TRAIL orders, not by our runner.

## A4. The trail conversion — why it worked but only barely

Activation level evolution across the holding period (recomputed each
day from `entry $312.67 + 2.0 × ATR_today`):

| Date | ATR | Activation | Close | Distance to activation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2026-05-11 | $7.45 | $327.57 | $312.75 | −$14.82 |
| 2026-05-12 | $7.23 | $327.13 | $312.25 | −$14.88 |
| 2026-05-13 | $6.96 | $326.58 | $312.58 | −$14.00 |
| 2026-05-14 | $6.73 | $326.13 | $314.88 | −$11.25 |
| 2026-05-15 | $6.69 | $326.04 | $320.74 | −$5.30 |
| **2026-05-18** | **$6.92** | **$326.51** | **$326.29** | **−$0.22** ← near-miss |
| **2026-05-19** | **$6.85** | **$326.51** | **$327.39** | **+$0.88** ← CONVERT |

The activation level *drifted up* on 2026-05-18 (ATR rose from $6.69 to
$6.92 over the high-volume day), which is what kept the 5/18 close
$326.29 *below* activation $326.51 — without that ATR drift, 5/18
would have crossed $326.04 cleanly and converted then. The 5/19
re-evaluation drifted the activation *back down* to $326.51 (ATR fell
to $6.85), and the 5/19 trail-manager observation $327.39 cleared it
by $0.88.

The conversion sequence:
- Trail manager runs 2026-05-19 at 14:09 UTC (10:09 ET — the daily run window)
- Reads current price $327.39 (Phase 2 partial-bar from yfinance at ~10:06 ET; **also** an intraday-quote inference, same pattern as the CLAUDE.md "Trail manager implicitly uses intraday quotes when Phase 2 refresh runs mid-morning" entry)
- Computes activation $326.51 from cost basis + 2.0×ATR — price is $0.88 above
- Cancel-TP → Cancel-STP → Submit-TRAIL with auxPrice $13.70 (2.0×ATR-at-conversion = $6.85 × 2.0)
- Initial trail trigger = $327.39 − $13.70 = $313.69 (above cost basis by $1.02)

**Observation that didn't exist before AON**: the initial trail trigger
is set by *the price at the moment of conversion*, not by activation.
If the price is barely above activation, the trail trigger lands
*right at* or just above entry. If the price is well above activation
(e.g. SNOW's $154.81 vs activation $150.83 = $3.98 above), the trail
trigger lands meaningfully above entry. AON's $0.88 over activation
locked in only $1.02 above entry; SNOW's $3.98 over activation locked
in $1.91 above entry; both small but the AON case is on the edge.

Ratchet evolution:
- 2026-05-19 intraday high $330.22 → trigger ratchets from $313.69 to $316.52
- 2026-05-20 high $323.60 → no new peak, trigger holds at $316.52
- 2026-05-20 low $316.90 → $0.38 above trigger (close call)
- 2026-05-21 open $318.71 → still above
- 2026-05-21 intraday → price crosses $316.52 → market sell fires

Peak-to-trigger gap stayed at exactly $13.70 throughout (by definition
of fixed-distance TRAIL). The peak-to-conversion-price gap of $2.83
($330.22 − $327.39) means **only $2.83 of additional gain was
ever available to be locked in by ratcheting** — that's why the
realised return is essentially the conversion-price-minus-trail
amount ($316.52 floor was set on 5/19 same morning as conversion;
nothing improved it before exit).

## A6. Skill vs luck retrospective

### A6.1 Did the system's risk machinery behave as designed?

**Yes — and the design exposed a structural narrowness on borderline
conversions.** The trail manager correctly skipped 8 consecutive
sub-activation days, correctly converted on the first above-activation
observation, and IBKR's TRAIL machinery correctly ratcheted on 5/19's
intraday peak and triggered on 5/21's morning gap-down. No bugs.

The design implication this trade exposed (which SNOW did not):

> **When `current_price` is only marginally above `activation_level`
> at conversion time, the initial trail trigger lands very close to
> entry. The trail distance ($13.70) is large enough that very little
> upside has to be given back before the trigger fires. AON converted
> $0.88 above activation, set the floor $1.02 above entry, peaked
> $2.83 above conversion, and exited at the ratcheted floor for +1.2%.**

This is *correctly* the design — the trail is supposed to protect
profits, not generate them — but it leaves an obvious question: **was
AON's BUY signal of any net value once the cost of execution and
realised P&L are honestly accounted?** Single trade, can't answer; but
the pattern argues for an enhancement.

### A6.2 Was the exit reasonable vs the post-trail-conversion peak?

Conversion 5/19: $327.39. Peak 5/19: $330.22 (same day). Exit
2026-05-21: $316.52.

| | Price | From entry | Captured |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conversion floor (5/19) | $313.69 (initial trail level = conversion price − $13.70) | +0.33% | The floor moved up by $2.83 between 5/19 morning conversion and 5/19 intraday peak, then froze |
| Peak (5/19 intraday) | $330.22 | +5.61% | "If we could have sold at the top" |
| Inferred exit (5/21 trail) | $316.52 | +1.23% | **21.9% of peak-move captured** — much worse than SNOW's 61% |
| Bracket TP (5/19 alt-history) | $336.73 (would have needed price to reach this) | +7.69% | Never tested — price never approached TP |

The peak-move-captured of 21.9% is **the structural floor for a
borderline conversion**: most of the entry-to-peak move was earned
*before* the conversion fired (entry $312.67 → conversion $327.39 =
+4.71%) and only $2.83 was added post-conversion (peak $330.22 −
conversion $327.39). The trail can only protect what was earned
*after* the conversion price; conversion happening late in the move
means the trail has very little to lock in.

If AON had peaked at e.g. $340 before the gap-down — a counterfactual
"the slow grind kept going" world — the trail would have ratcheted to
$326.30, exit would have been ~+4.4%, and the trade would have looked
much better. AON didn't peak much above the conversion because the
move that drove the conversion was itself the peak.

### A6.3 Counterfactuals

1. **Conversion buffer (`daily_runner_conversion_buffer_atr` = 0.5,
   mirroring the existing `intraday_conversion_buffer_atr`)**. Would
   have required `current_price >= entry + (2.0 + 0.5) × ATR` to
   convert. On 5/19: $312.67 + 2.5 × $6.85 = $329.80. AON's 5/19
   trail-manager observation $327.39 falls *below* this — no conversion.
   The bracket TP $336.73 stays live; the bracket stop $297.84 stays
   live. On 5/20 close $321.15 and 5/21 morning, the bracket TP at
   $336.73 was never approached; the bracket stop $297.84 was never
   approached either. **AON would still be held today at $322.73.**
   Whether that's a better outcome depends on what AON does next —
   if it pulls back to $315 over the next week, the bracket stop would
   give a slightly worse exit than the trail's $316.52. If it rallies
   to $340, the bracket TP would give a much better exit.
2. **`trailing_stop_trail_atr` 2.0 → 1.0** (tighter trail). Trail
   distance $6.85. Initial trail level = $327.39 − $6.85 = $320.54
   (above cost basis by $7.87). Peak ratchets trigger to $330.22 − $6.85
   = $323.37. 5/20 low $316.90 traversed $320.54 *before* the trail
   could ratchet to $323.37 → exit on 5/20 at ~$320.54, realising +2.5%.
   **Better than the realised +1.2% — by ~$167**. But this is a
   knob change that hurts SNOW's outcome (§12.3 showed SNOW at trail=1.0
   exits at +14.2% instead of +17.5% — worse by ~$1,000). The 2.0×
   default is a deliberate tradeoff.
3. **Hold-timeout (`hold_timeout_enabled=True`, max=30 days)**. Not
   applicable here — AON held only 13 calendar days, well inside any
   reasonable timeout, and AON had a passed-gate BUY at entry that
   stayed within the hold window the whole time. Even if Phase 3.6
   were on, AON would not have been flattened by it.

### A6.4 One-sentence read

**AON is the system's first "borderline conversion": the trail did
its job correctly, but the conversion fired so close to the activation
threshold that the floor barely cleared entry and only $2.83 of
post-conversion upside was available to be locked in — net +1.2%.**
The system worked; the trade itself was thin. The structural fix that
n=1 suggests but doesn't yet justify is a conversion buffer on the
daily-runner path (already exists for the intraday-runner path).
