the exit-day OHLCV bar of a *rotated-out, long-held* position (AXTI, GEV) is left as a

members whose bars were refresh-eligible (ON, NXPI) show the stop fill inside their 6/09

ambient market, not yet a drawdown).

## 3. The model never exited any of them — the stop was the sole backstop

The cross-case signal-trail finding. `signal_log` ensemble + LSTM across each hold:

| Symbol | LSTM trajectory | Ensemble trajectory | Ever SELL? | Signal on 6/09 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| AXTI | pinned **0.99→0.999** entire hold | BUY/HOLD, re-fired BUY 6/04–6/09 | **No** | BUY (rejected, sector) |
| NXPI | pinned **0.999** entire hold | BUY→HOLD (0.53–0.57) | **No** | HOLD |
| ON | **0.96→0.99** | BUY→HOLD (0.52) | **No** | HOLD |
| GEV | swung 0.69→−0.84→+0.91 (XGBoost pinned bullish 0.80–0.97) | BUY most of hold, HOLD only 6/08–6/09 | **No** | HOLD |

**None of the four ever crossed the SELL gate.** Three (AXTI/NXPI/ON) had LSTM saturated
bullish (≥0.96) the entire way down — the price model was maximally wrong and never
wavered. GEV's LSTM oscillated but XGBoost stayed pinned bullish, so the ensemble held
BUY/HOLD throughout. The consequence is uniform: **with no SELL signal, the only exit any
of these positions had was the bracket ATR stop.** Phase 3.5 (trailing) never engaged —
every trail evaluation was SKIPPED (price never reached the inflated activation level on
these volatile names). Phase 3.6 (hold-timeout) is off by default and, even if on, keys on
"most recent passed-gate BUY" — AXTI/GEV re-fired BUYs during the bleed, which would have
reset the staleness clock. So the −2×ATR stop did 100% of the exit work, and on high-ATR
names that is a wide stop: −22% (AXTI), −11% (ON), −9% (GEV), −8% (NXPI).

This is the **same structural gap** the CLAUDE.md "LSTM-saturated-bearish held longs have
no exit but the ATR stop" enhancement (the AZN pattern) describes — here with the LSTM
saturated *bullish* on falling longs (inverse sign, identical mechanism: saturated price
model + ensemble never crosses the SELL gate ⇒ only the ATR stop exits). The losers cluster
takes this finding from the single AZN observation to **n≈3–4**.

## 4. Skill vs luck retrospective

### 4a. Did the risk machinery behave as designed?

**Yes, and it is the only reason the cluster cost <2% of the book.** Each stop was placed
at entry at −2×ATR, sized so the position was ~$40k (~3.8% of NLV). When the cohort rolled
over, each stop fired at its designed level. The blended −12.6% cluster loss was contained
to −1.9% of NLV precisely because position sizing capped each name at ~3.8% — four
simultaneous −8% to −22% single-name losses summed to a survivable book hit. **The stop
and the sizer did their jobs.** What's absent is any *earlier* exit: the model offered none,
so the stop was the first and only line.

### 4b. Were the exits reasonable vs the pre-exit highs?

These are stops, so by construction they exit near the local low — the relevant question is
whether an earlier, system-feasible exit existed. For NXPI and ON the answer is essentially
no: both were short holds (4 and 2 days) that went almost straight down from entry into the
stop, with the model BUY/HOLD throughout — there was no signal-feasible exit between entry
and stop. GEV and AXTI were longer holds (16 and 13 days) that *did* spend time in profit
(GEV peaked ~$1095 on 5/26, +10%; AXTI peaked $141 on 5/22, +26%) before bleeding to the
stop — for those two, the **trailing stop is the system-feasible exit that didn't fire**,
which is the AXTI §8f "expanding-ATR activation chase" finding (the activation level rose
with volatility and stayed ahead of the price). GEV is a second instance of that: it never
cleared its trail activation despite a +10% peak.

### 4c. Counterfactuals

1. **Tighter correlation cap / sector-aware sizing.** The cluster is a single-factor event;
   a guard that recognized "4th high-beta-semi long" and either blocked the entry or
   half-sized it would have reduced the aggregate hit. But the 30% sector cap (Technology)
   *did* start biting on these names by 6/04–6/08 (NXPI/ON entries' later adds were
   sector-rejected) — the cap limits *new* exposure but does nothing about the *already-held*
   correlated book bleeding together. Tradeoff: a tighter correlation cap also blocks
   participation in a cohort *rally* (the inverse of this event), and the AXTI April winner
   came from exactly this cohort.
2. **Conviction-override SELL on saturated-LSTM-wrong-way.** If the gate forced a re-evaluation
   when LSTM saturates against a losing position, NXPI/ON/AXTI would have had a model exit
   above their stops. But these had LSTM saturated *bullish* while falling — the override
   that helps here ("LSTM stuck bullish + position down >X% ⇒ exit") is a drawdown guard, not
   a model-signal rule, and risks cutting winners that dip-then-run (the AXTI April winner dipped
   to −5% intraday on entry day before ripping).
3. **Lower trail activation / first-target-becomes-trail (GEV, AXTI only).** Would have
   captured part of GEV's +10% and AXTI's +26% peaks before the reversal — the standing AXTI
   §6 intervention space. Does nothing for NXPI/ON (never had a peak to trail).

### 4d. One-sentence read

**Structural, not bad luck:** four correctly-sized, correctly-stopped longs in one high-beta
cohort bled together through a risk-off stretch with no model exit available, so the ATR stop
— the system's only backstop when the model stays bullish into a drawdown — fired on all four
at its designed wide level; the sizing discipline is what kept a −12.6% cluster to −1.9% of
the book.

### 5a. New finding — exit-day bar of a rotated-out, long-held position is stale until the next EOD after reconciliation

The sharpest new mechanism from this cluster. **Two of the four stop exits (AXTI, GEV) sit
at fill prices below the lowest cached daily low for their entire holding period** — i.e.
the cached bars say the stop was never reached, yet the stop filled:

| Symbol | Stop | Exit fill | 6/09 cached daily low | Lowest cached daily low in hold | Refresh-eligible on 6/09? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| ON | $108.37 | $108.30 | **$108.30** (= fill) | $108.30 | ✅ active in universe → Phase 2 refresh |
| NXPI | $295.90 | $295.75 | **$279.36** (< stop) | $279.36 | ✅ APPROVED 6/03 (≤14d) → recently-acted refresh |
| **AXTI** | $87.06 | $87.07 | $91.48 (> fill) | $87.27 (still > fill) | ❌ removed 6/07 + last APPROVED 5/21 (>14d) |
| **GEV** | $902.74 | $902.58 | $921.00 (> fill) | $921.00 (> fill) | ❌ removed 5/24 + last APPROVED 5/18 (>14d) |

The differential is fully explained by `refresh_recent_bars.py`'s three union sources
(active universe ∪ recently-acted-APPROVED-within-14d ∪ currently-held/recently-exited).
ON (active) and NXPI (APPROVED 6/03, within 14d) qualify, so their 6/09 bars were finalized
post-close and correctly show the stop inside the range. AXTI and GEV qualify for **none** of
the three on 6/09: rotated out of the universe, last APPROVED >14 trading days prior, and —
because they *exited intraday 6/09* — no longer held by that evening's EOD run, while their
`trade_log` `source='live'` exit row didn't exist until the Flex reconciliation the next
morning (6/10). So their 6/09 bar remained the **stale ~10am partial** written by the 6/09
signal_runner Phase 2 fetch, recorded *before* the stop fired intraday — which is exactly why
it sits above the fill. (All four generated only HOLD/BUY on 6/09 — no SELL/CLOSED_LONG — so
the fills were unambiguously bracket-stop fills, not market closes; the ≈-to-the-penny match
to each stop confirms it.)

**Self-heals:** once the 6/10 Flex reconciliation wrote the AXTI/GEV exit rows, they entered
`_recently_exited_symbols` (trade_log `source='live'` exit within 14d), so the *next* EOD run
(2026-06-10 16:30 ET, `days_back=5` covering 6/05–6/10) will overwrite the stale 6/09 bars
with finalized values. The defect is therefore a **~1-day forensic-timing window** — but
within that window (which is exactly when the daily review / a case study runs, the morning
after the exit) stop-exit reconstruction is actively misleading: the cached data contradicts
the recorded stop.

```
- **Exit-day OHLCV bar of a rotated-out, long-held position is left stale until the next EOD run** *(noted 2026-06-10 — forensic-timing, self-healing)* (`scripts/refresh_recent_bars.py`, `data/fetcher.py:_phase2_data_refresh`): when a position that has (a) rotated out of the active universe AND (b) whose last APPROVED `order_decisions` row is >14 trading days old exits intraday, its exit-day bar is never finalized that evening — on the exit day the symbol is in none of `refresh_recent_bars`' three union sources (not active-universe, not recently-acted-APPROVED-within-14d, and no `trade_log` `source='live'` exit row yet because Flex reconciliation runs the *next* morning, T+1). So the exit-day bar stays the stale ~10am partial written by that day's signal_runner Phase 2 fetch — recorded *before* the stop fired — and shows a daily low *above* the stop fill, contradicting the recorded exit. **Observed 2026-06-09:** AXTI (stop $87.06, fill $87.07, but 6/09 cached low $91.48) and GEV (stop $902.74, fill $902.58, but 6/09 cached low $921.00 — and no daily bar in the entire hold reached the stop) both stopped out below their lowest cached daily low; the two cluster members that WERE refresh-eligible (ON active-in-universe; NXPI APPROVED 6/03 ≤14d) show the stop fill correctly inside their finalized 6/09 range. Self-heals the next EOD run after reconciliation writes the exit row (the symbol then enters `_recently_exited_symbols`, days_back=5 overwrites the stale bar), so the impact is a ~1-day window — but that window is exactly when the daily review / a case study runs, making stop-exit forensics misleading. **Fix / proposed change:** in `reconcile_fills` (or the daily runner after Phase 1), force a `refresh_recent(symbol, days_back=N)` for any symbol that just received a `source='live'` exit row, so the exit-day bar is finalized in the same run that records the trade rather than waiting for that evening's EOD; alternatively widen `_recently_acted_symbols` to include rotated-out names with an open `fill_log` entry-only position. **Trigger to revisit:** already n=2 (AXTI, GEV) — promote from "noted" to "fix" if a 3rd rotated-out long-held stop exit lands forensically-misleading, OR if any review draws a *wrong conclusion* (not just a flagged caveat) from a stale exit-day bar.
```

This supersedes the narrower n=1 "Flex `exit_ts` quirk" framing in the 2026-06-10 followup
(`docs/reviews/followups.md`) — the cluster shows it's a stale-bar / refresh-eligibility gap,
not a Flex timestamp problem, and GEV is the 2nd instance that the followup's escalation
criterion ("a 2nd live stop fill below all cached daily lows") was waiting for.

**Update (2026-06-10) — fix shipped + validated.** Implemented as proposed: `ReconcileResult`
gained an `exited_symbols` set (populated only on a real `_write_round_trip`), and both
daily-automation reconcilers (`signal_runner` Phase 1 + `scripts/reconcile_flex` Step 3c) now
call `refresh_recent_bars.refresh_symbols(result.exited_symbols)` immediately after
reconciliation, finalising the exit-day bar in the same run that records the trade. 3 new
tests (`tests/test_reconciliation.py::TestExitedSymbolsTracking`); full suite 444 passed.
**End-to-end validation:** running the new `refresh_symbols(['AXTI','GEV'])` against the
production DB corrected the two stale 6/09 bars — **AXTI low $91.48 → $76.00** and **GEV
$921.00 → $878.00** — both now below their stops ($87.06 / $902.74), confirming the
stale-bar diagnosis to the penny and resolving the 6/09 forensic discrepancy. Now tracked as
a CLAUDE.md "Outstanding bugs" entry (*code complete 2026-06-10 — awaiting live verification*);

| **ARM** | bullish throughout | No (HOLD) | trailing (converted 6/18 @ +22%) | **+0.5% — trail gave back essentially the entire +22% run** |

Two distinct lessons fall out:

**(a) The model's SELL is the only *tight* exit — and it only fired on CSCO.** CSCO is the
counter-example to Part I's "the model never exits": its LSTM flipped hard (1.0→−0.96) three days after
entry and the ensemble crossed the SELL gate on 6/22, closing the position at −1.0% — an order of
magnitude smaller loss than the three stop-outs. The other three losers replay Part I exactly: LSTM
stayed bullish (SLV *saturated* at 0.96–0.98 the whole way down, the classic AZN/6-09 pattern),
ensemble never crossed SELL, so the −2×ATR stop was the sole exit — and on these names that is a −12%
to −15% exit by construction. This takes the "saturated-LSTM-bullish-into-decline → stop-only exit"
finding from Part I's n≈4 to **n≈6–7** (adds ORCL, SATS, SLV; CSCO is the n=1 *exception* where the
model did its job).

**(b) The 2×ATR trail, once converted, gave back most of a 20%+ peak on both names that triggered it.**
ARM converted 6/18 at $446.67 (+22% over the $365.80 entry) with a trail distance of **$69.90
(2×ATR, ATR≈$35 — ~16% of price)**; MU converted 6/22 at $1,193.63 (+20%) with a **$158.30** trail
distance (ATR≈$79). Because the trail distance scales with ATR and these are high-ATR names, the trail
sat so far below the high-water mark that ARM round-tripped +22%→+0.5% and MU +20%→+6% before the
stop triggered. The trail *engaged correctly* (Phase 3.5 converted, then ratcheted the trigger up on
the RATCHETED rows) — the giveback is purely the **width** of the 2×ATR distance, not a failure to fire.

## II.4 Skill vs luck retrospective

**II.4a Did the risk machinery behave as designed?** Yes, uniformly. Every stop fired at its −2×ATR
level; both trails converted at the correct activation and ratcheted; CSCO's gate flipped and closed
the long; sizing capped each name at ~3.8% so the −$16.5k of losses was −1.6% of the ~$1.04M book and
was nearly offset by the +$13.9k of batch winners. Nothing malfunctioned. What's *absent* is any tight
exit on the three stop names — the model offered none, so the wide stop was the first and only line.

**II.4b Were the exits reasonable vs a system-feasible alternative?** 
- **SATS** went down from the entry day (no peak to protect) — there was no feasible better exit; the
  stop is the floor. *(The −13.7% entry was simply a bad fill into a falling knife — SATS dropped
  131→106 intraday on 6/12, and the bracket filled at $116.50 on the way down.)*
- **ORCL / SLV** both peaked early (+8% / +7% on 6/15) then bled to the stop with the model bullish the
  whole way. The system-feasible exit that did NOT fire is the trailing stop — but neither cleared its
  activation (entry + 2×ATR), the same "expanding-ATR activation chase" as Part I §4b. The +7–8% peaks
  were well short of the ~+13–16% (2×ATR) activation thresholds.
- **ARM / MU** are the inverse problem: the trail *did* engage, but gave back the gain. A system-feasible
  better exit existed (a tighter trail, or a first-target partial) — see counterfactuals.
- **CSCO** exited at −1.0% via the model SELL — close to the best feasible outcome given it reversed.

**II.4c Counterfactuals.**
1. **Tighter trail distance (`trailing_stop_trail_atr` 2.0 → 1.0–1.5).** ARM's $446.67 conversion with a
   1×ATR ($35) trail would have exited ~$412 (+12.6%) instead of +0.5%; MU's 1×ATR ($79) trail exits
   ~$1,115 (+12%) instead of +6%. **Net: +$4.4k more captured across the two.** Tradeoff: a 1×ATR trail
   also stops out earlier on a normal pullback-then-continue, so it would clip winners that wobble — this
   is exactly the `trail_vs_tp_capture.md` H3 parameter-grid question, and ARM/MU are now two clean live
   data points for it (n=2 trail conversions, both gave back >half the peak).
2. **First-target-becomes-trail / scale-out at +1×ATR.** Selling half at the TP-halfway point and trailing
   the rest would have locked part of ARM's +22% and MU's +20% regardless of the wide trail. Helps the
   peak-then-reverse names (ARM/MU/ORCL/SLV); does nothing for SATS (no peak).
3. **Conviction-override SELL on saturated-LSTM.** Would help only where LSTM stays *bullish into a
   decline* (SLV especially, pinned 0.96–0.98 the whole drop). But CSCO shows the model *can* flip on its
   own when the reversal is real — so an override risks pre-empting the gate's own (cheaper) exit. Same
   deferral as Part I §4c-2: decide on realised P&L once Phase B has enough saturated-held exits.

**II.4d One-sentence read.** Twelve identically-screened longs entered on one day dispersed across a
±36-point realised range driven *entirely by exit mechanism* — the model's SELL gave the only tight
exit (CSCO −1%), the wide ATR stop turned three model-silent reversals into −12% to −15% exits
(ORCL/SATS/SLV), and the wide 2×ATR trail handed back 20-point peaks to near-breakeven on the two names
it did protect (ARM/MU) — so the lesson is not "bad entries" but "**when the model stays bullish, every
exit the system has left is an ATR-wide move**."
